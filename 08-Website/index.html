01

Older than chess.
Pure as mathematics.

Kharbga descends from the Roman Ludus Latrunculorum — "the game of soldiers" — played by legionaries across the empire. For centuries it lived on in the sand of southern Tunisia, drawn with a stick, played with stones.

It belongs to the same noble family as chess and Go: no dice, no cards, no chance. You win by thinking further than your rival. We've kept that soul exactly — and added a modern edge.

02

How it works

Move

Slide a soldier one square — up, down, left or right. Never diagonally. Simple to learn.

Surround

Trap an enemy between two of your soldiers on a line, and it falls. The heart of Kharbga.

Chain

One capture lets the same soldier strike again — build a combo and clear a whole line.

Crown

Seven captures and your soldier is promoted to a Centurion that rules the board.

2,500 years,
one game.

530 BCE

A Greek vase shows Achilles at the board — Petteia, the ancestor.

1st c. BCE

Rome plays Ludus Latrunculorum, "the game of soldiers," from Hadrian's Wall to Carthage.

11th c.

Carried across North Africa, it takes root in the Tunisian south — Tataouine, Gafsa, Djerba.

Today

Elders still draw it in the sand. Now reborn by Kogia Games for the world.
